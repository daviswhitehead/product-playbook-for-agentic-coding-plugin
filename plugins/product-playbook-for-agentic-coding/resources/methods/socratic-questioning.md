# Socratic Questioning

## What It Is

A 5-category questioning framework for validating proposals, PRDs, and strategic decisions. Forces clarity by surfacing assumptions through targeted questions.

## When to Use

- Validating a PRD or proposal before committing to build
- During product discovery when problem/solution clarity is uncertain
- Reviewing a strategy document for logical gaps
- Any time a decision feels "obvious" but hasn't been pressure-tested

## The Framework

### Category 1: Problem Clarity

- What specific pain does the user experience today?
- Who experiences this pain most acutely? How many of them?
- What is the cost of inaction — what happens if we do nothing?
- How do users solve this problem today without our product?
- What evidence do we have that this is a real problem (not an assumed one)?

### Category 2: Solution Validation

- Why this solution over alternatives?
- What is the simplest version that solves the core problem?
- What user behavior are we assuming will change?
- What has to be true for this solution to work?
- Have we seen this approach succeed elsewhere? In what context?

### Category 3: Success Criteria

- How will we measure success? What is the primary metric?
- What does failure look like — what would make us kill this?
- What is the target within what timeframe?
- Are we measuring outcomes (user behavior changed) or outputs (feature shipped)?

### Category 4: Constraints

- What are the top 3 technical risks?
- What are we explicitly not doing?
- If we had half the time, what would we cut?
- What external dependencies could block us?
- What regulatory, legal, or compliance constraints apply?

### Category 5: Strategic Fit

- Why now? What changed that makes this the right time?
- How does this connect to the company's current strategy?
- What does this enable or prevent in the future?
- What are we saying no to by saying yes to this?

## How to Apply

1. Pick 3-5 questions most relevant to the current artifact. Quality of questioning matters more than coverage.
2. For each question, assess whether the artifact provides a clear answer.
3. Flag questions where the answer is absent, vague, or assumed.
4. Use flagged gaps as discussion points or revision targets.

Do not ask all 20+ questions — that's an interrogation, not a review.
